Getting Started with Your BitAxe Miner

July 2026 · 8 min read

What is a BitAxe?

The BitAxe is an open-source Bitcoin ASIC miner designed for solo mining and educational purposes. Built around the BM1397 chip (the same used in Antminer S9), it offers a compact, energy-efficient way to participate in Bitcoin mining at home.

Unlike industrial miners, the BitAxe is designed to be quiet, energy-efficient, and accessible. It connects to your home WiFi and can be managed entirely through a web interface — no special software required.

Unboxing and First Boot

When you receive your BitAxe, you'll find:

  • The BitAxe board with heatsink and fan
  • A USB-C power cable
  • A quick-start card with the default IP address

Connect the power supply and plug it in. The fan will spin up, and within 30 seconds, the LED should indicate the miner is ready. Connect your phone or computer to the same WiFi network and navigate to the miner's IP address in a browser.

Initial Configuration

Once connected to the web interface, you'll want to configure these settings immediately:

  • WiFi credentials — Enter your home WiFi SSID and password
  • Pool URL — Connect to a mining pool (we recommend starting with Braiins Pool or Solo Satoshi)
  • Miner name — Give your miner a memorable name like "Living Room BitAxe"

After saving, the miner will reboot and begin hashing. You should see the hashrate stabilize around 1.0–1.5 TH/s within a few minutes.

Optimizing Performance

Once your miner is running, here are tips to get the most out of it:

Temperature Management

The ideal operating temperature for the BM1397 is between 55°C and 75°C. Above 80°C, the chip throttles. Ensure good airflow around the heatsink and consider adding a small desk fan if ambient temperatures are high.

Frequency Tuning

The default frequency works well for most setups, but you can experiment with slight adjustments. Higher frequency = more hashrate but also more heat and power draw. Find the sweet spot where efficiency (J/TH) is minimized.

Network Stability

A stable WiFi connection is critical. If your miner drops shares or disconnects frequently, consider:

  • Moving the miner closer to your router
  • Using a 2.4GHz network (better range than 5GHz)
  • Connecting via Ethernet if possible

Common Issues and Fixes

  • Low hashrate: Check temperature and ensure the fan is spinning. Clean dust from the heatsink.
  • High share rejection: Usually indicates network latency. Try a pool server closer to your location.
  • Miner offline: Power cycle the miner. If it persists, check WiFi credentials and signal strength.
  • Fan noise: Normal under load, but if excessively loud, the bearing may need replacement.
